I — Abstract

Abstract

On a cloudless April afternoon, I went to Praça Francisco de Morais with six microphones to record the sound of a pigeon. The square is named after the sixteenth-century author of the chivalric romance Palmerin of England.1 Almost five centuries separate that novel from my recording, yet their contents share a structural logic: the pursuit of the encounter and the desire to capture.

This project lets the grammar of field recording discourse and that of the chivalric romance leak into one another, allowing their logics to expose one another's failures. It asks: what does the failure to capture reveal about the logic embedded in field recording discourse, and what can speculative writing offer in its place?

My argument is not that individual acts of field recording are harmful by definition, but that the grammar of capture influences performed practice, and that finding alternatives to that grammar is an important part of field recording practice. The case study proposes methodological failure as the framework: a binaural recording of a failed field trip, accompanied by a booklet of diary entries interleaved with pages from Palmerin of England.

II — Introduction

Introduction

This research draws from a personal necessity: I am a student funded to travel through Europe to learn about sound, and at the core of this practice is a need to continue working guided by what Donna Haraway (1988) calls situated knowledges:2 the understanding that all knowledge is produced from an embodied position, and that acknowledging this makes rich and responsible knowledge-making possible.

My argument is not that individual acts of field recording are harmful by definition, but that the grammar of capture influences performed practice, and that finding alternatives to that grammar is an important part of field recording practice. Alternatives are already being proposed from within the discourse: M.P. Wright (2022)3 suggests this grammar is not settled, and proposes a different vocabulary in its place: from capture to inhabit, from pursue to participate.

IV — Literature Review

The Grammar of Capture

Medieval romances about encounters with the feisty foreign and ten-eyed monstrous were not passive reflections of social ideals but, as Jaeger (1985)4 argues, actively shaped them, blurring the distinction between literary imagination and performed practice. The structural grammar of the knight in unknown territory attempting to capture leaks from the chivalric romance into the colonial explorer, into the natural history expedition, into the field recordist. Not because these acts are equivalent, but because they share an inherited vocabulary that pre-shapes the encounter before it begins.

The field recording discourse this research draws from is, in Wright's (2022, p. 2)3 words, an exploration of the gap worlds of humans and nonhumans through the capture, construction, and mediation of sound. Its grammar favours the recordist as neutral witness to an acoustic world that exists independently of the act of recording it: as if the sound were there to be found rather than co-created. Mary Louise Pratt's (1992)5 concept of the contact zone, a space where different histories joust under asymmetrical conditions, frames the recording location as already charged before the record button is pressed. Wright (2022)3 makes the same point from a different angle: tactless field recording is the failure to attend to the encounter as an encounter, a lack of relational awareness toward environment and nonhuman others.

Speculative Writing

The trap: writing about field recording risks the same taxonomic closure as the act itself. Haraway's (2016)6 concept of metaphorisation, the active engagement with how language re-figures worlds rather than illustrating pre-existing ideas, offers an orientation for language that is relational rather than extractive.

Susan Sontag (1977)7 tells us that to capture a moment is to participate in its dying: every photograph is a certificate of presence that simultaneously announces an absence. A recording does capture a sound, but not the complexity of the encounter that created it. The shift from capture to inhabit is not a moral correction but a reorientation: to stay inside the encounter rather than extract from it.

Ursula K. Le Guin's The Author of the Acacia Seeds (1974)8 proposes therolinguistics: a speculative pseudo-academic writing that imagines nonhuman writing systems, demonstrating that speculation does not oppose serious study. 'We venture to suggest that the confusion over Seed 31 may result from an ethnocentric interpretation of the word "up"' (Le Guin, 1974/2012, p. 2). This performs exactly what Haraway means by metaphorisation: language that imagines different sets of relations and worlds, rather than confirming those already known.

Self-Erasure and Self-Performance

The dominant presentation of the I in field recording is non-presentation: the recordist as a ghost, the microphone as a transparent window into the real. Jonathan Sterne (2003)9 argues that recording technologies were built around a logic of separating sound from its source. The directional microphone extends this logic: it isolates a sound object from its environment, removing the traces of the encounter that produced it.

To claim self-erasure is to claim what Haraway (1988)2 calls the god-trick: the pretence of objectivity that conceals its own situatedness. M.P. Wright's (2022)3 figure of the Noisy Nonself turns self-erasure into self-performance: a monstrous, bigfoot-like persona whose windshield fur makes visible what is normally suppressed: the technology and the recordist as co-creators of the recorded material. This case study is situated at a similar staging, but with a different approach: rather than making the means of capture visible through an embodied persona, it makes them visible through documented failure.

V — Case Study

Palmerins and Pigeonholes

The three literature threads meet here: the grammar of capture shapes the encounter, language can re-figure rather than just describe, and the recordist's presence is never neutral.

The artistic work was presented as an installation at Jardim do Bombarda. It included a booklet with diary pages interleaved with pages from Palmerin of England, and wireless headphones playing a binaural recording of the journey to the square. The binaural format places the listener inside the recordist's spatial position, hearing the equipment being sorted in my bedroom, as if it were the listener themselves getting ready to go out and record. This is a way of actively refusing the god-trick: making the recordist present rather than a ghost.

None of the other microphones brought to the square are heard in the sound piece. A separate shotgun recording, made on a different evening, was placed centre in the mix. The listener can hear different dogs running around in the square; people keep greeting the same dog: Hiu. The name means shark in another language. He glides around the square, becoming a dogfish through language. The dog's tongue gets bitten by a mosquito, who zooms past the microphone with a buzzing question: does the hand grip the microphone, or does the microphone grip the hand? This attempt at metaphorisation reshapes what is present in the square; renaming transforms the dog into a predator, and the mosquito's question reverses the grammar of capture: subject and object switch places.

VI — Discussion, Future Research & References

What Does the Puddle Reflect?

The unsuccessful field trip yielded three things a conventionally successful one could not have. First, the ambient distance: traffic, wind, the square's environment, fiddling with equipment. What is heard is not the pigeon but the conditions under which the pigeon was pursued. Second, the diary entries expanded only because the encounter failed. There was nothing to document, so the writing turned toward the framework: the set of assumptions carried into the field, that a successful field recording means capturing a clear, isolated sound of a target subject. The broken windshield, scaring the pigeon, writing about its inaudible wing flaps, rethinking the approach, all of it makes the expectation visible as a singular objective. If this is not achievable, then: 'What am I doing here?' Third, the failure exposed the intention as its own contradiction: a critique of capture organised around a desire to capture. A clean take confirms the framework rather than revealing it. The booklet does not document the pigeon but documents the impossibility of documenting the pigeon, and in doing so makes audible what the recording alone could not.

Haraway's (2016) antidote to paralysis is not a checklist of principles but a cultivation of the capacity to respond: to be open, accountable, and to allow the encounter to make demands the framework did not anticipate.

The booklet form, combining diary and romance, enacts a version of Haraway's cat's cradle: a string game that can only be played by at least two parties, where no single player authors the pattern and each move inherits the shape left by the other. In this case study, patterns inherited from the chivalric tradition and the field recording practice are held alongside each other and passed on in a changed form.

Future Research

What does the puddle in the cornfield reflect? In this project, it is the space that opens when the singular objective flies away. Not an absence of successful capture but an alternative approach: another attentiveness, another grammar. The puddle may reflect many more imaginaries, and I wonder what future field recording practice, based on attentiveness toward this puddle, might look like.

This is only a small part of my interest in sound ecology, SF: speculative fabulation, interspecies listening, and ecofiction. I look forward to reading, listening, and experiencing more of the work of Ludwig Berger, Pablo Diserens, Salomé Voegelin, Donna Haraway, Mark Peter Wright, and many more, to see how they influence my multi-media practice.
